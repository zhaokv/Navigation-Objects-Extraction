EXCLUSIVE:THE PEOPLE REVEALS THE NHS BLUEPRINT DESIGNED TO REVOLUTIONISE OUR WARDS
Private room for EVERY patient En-suite bathroom, TV & web It's prototype for all hospitals
By Nigel Nelson

All NHS patients are to be offered their own hotel-style ensuite bedrooms in a healthcare revolution, The People can reveal.

Medical chiefs believe scrapping open-plan wards will help stop the spread of killer superbugs such as MRSA.

Private rooms will give patients more privacy and dignity - and let them talk to doctors without other people earwigging.

The elderly and frail will face fewer risks of falling because loos will be much closer.

And each room will have internet access, TV, radio and phone.

The blueprint for the future is to be tested at Hillingdon Hospital, west London, where 24 single ensuite rooms are being built.

The complex, known as the Bevan ward, will cost about £3million and is due to be opened in December.

If the pilot scheme works, the plan to give a single room to every patient who wants one could be introduced across the country.

But it will take decades to bring in fully because the NHS currently runs 1,400 hospital in Britain. NHS minister and former nurse Ann Keen said: "The outcome of the Hillingdon project will be a major factor in decisions for all new facilities.

"There can be no way single rooms are not beneficial - I would have wanted the opportunity to nurse like that.

"This is the way forward for both patients AND staff.

"And I'm positive that's the way we will go."

She added: "Learning from the pilot will be transferable across the whole NHS.

"This will give patients much more dignity in hospitals.

"And nurses are always delighted for the opportunity to give real quality care to their patients."

Health Secretary Alan Johnson is so convinced of the benefits he has already ordered £238million to be spent on a new 512-bed hospital in Pembury, Kent.

All the wards except intensive care, maternity and other specialist units will be made up of single ensuite bedrooms when it opens in two years time.

Project boss Graham Goddard said: "It will be the safest hospital in the country - and will set a new benchmark for the NHS."

The cost of the 24-bed Bevan ward is being shared by the Hillingdon Hospital Trust, the Government and private sponsorship.

Despite its price tag, bosses insist the day-to-day running costs of single rooms will be LOWER than in old-fashioned open-plan wards.

Marie Batey, Hillingdon Hospital's Director of Nursing, said: "It won't require any more staff and it should actually free up nurses' and doctors' time because they will be closer to patients."

And she said it would improve one-to-one care - which should mean patients make quicker recoveries.

There are fears some patients could be left feeling isolated by single rooms.

But designers are looking at ways to make sure there WILL be opportunities for patients to socialise with one another.

Each cluster of rooms could have a corridor wide enough to allow space for chairs and coffee tables so they can meet up and chat.

And there may be a central area containing a communal kitchen as well as the reception desk.

Across Britain, the proportion of single rooms in NHS hospitals has risen from just under a quarter five years ago to almost a third now.

And health chiefs have ordered all new hospitals - as well as any older ones being refurbished - to provide more single rooms than the facilities they are replacing.

Each new room costs an average of £125,800 to set up. But the real challenge for NHS planners is to bring them in while maintaining the existing bed capacity.

A key element in the Hillingdon prototype is to find out if money can be saved by speeding up recovery rates so more patients can be admitted for treatment - and help waiting lists tumble.

A team of academics from York University will also look at whether noise levels fall with individual rooms.

And they will measure predicted improvements in sleep quality.

At the end of their stay, patients will be asked to rate the hospital - just as they would in hotel customer satisfaction surveys.

Staff will be monitored to see if the distance they walk during a shift goes down - and if their workloads are reduced.

And medical experts will study the impact of single rooms on MRSA and other easily-spread infections which have plagued NHS complexes for decades.

Architects advising the Department of Health said SIX years ago private rooms should replace traditional hospital wards.

And they asked ministers only to approve new hospital projects which included single-room plans.

They were backed by the Royal College of Nursing, who said: "We often underestimate the value of sleep for health and recuperation."
