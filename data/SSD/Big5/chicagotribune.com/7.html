Before diving into a triathlon, swim in the lake

Julie Deardorff |  
July 8, 2007

There's nothing creepier than running into something while swimming in deep, murky Lake Michigan, even if it's just a plastic bag.

But if you're signed up for the Swim Across America event on July 21 or next month's Accenture Chicago Triathlon, don't make a common rookie mistake by waiting until race day to plunge into the great lake.

E. coli-related beach closings and motorboat fumes aside, open-water swimming can be exhilarating. Intrepid swimmers have established a course along the lake wall at Ohio Street Beach, and although wetsuits provide buoyancy and warmth, they're not essential in July and August when water temperatures range from 65 to 75 degrees.

But beginners and even advanced triathletes often do all their training in the cozy confines of a pool. During the race they realize that the turbulent lake is a different beast: There are no lane lines, it's impossible to see anything, and the thrill of the event can take your breath away. "Swimming can be learned, but the process can be difficult, tedious and anxiety-provoking, so many people avoid it," said Drew Surinsky, a triathlete, swim coach and sport physiologist at Lake Forest Hospital. "Unfortunately, fitness and conditioning from another sport don't simply transfer over to swimming." Then there are psychological hurdles, something even all-American athletes face. "I used to dread open-water swimming," confessed Ironman triathlete and triathlon coach Jennifer Harrison, who, despite a swimming background, worried that a sea creature would bite her.

Still, Harrison, of West Dundee, realized that if she never become "one" with the water, she was never going to get faster. "So I embraced it and kept my mind in the game," she said. "The body followed."

That doesn't mean a strong mind will keep you afloat when your goggles get kicked off your face or you swallow a wave. But Harrison, who has been hit in the head, pulled under and had her feet grabbed, tells her clients to expect to get beaten up in the water. She suggests practicing by having friends pull and tug at you in the open water so it's not a shock on race day.

Other open-water swim tips:

* Start slowly, stay calm and start in the back if you're a beginner. "Let the commotion go by and then get in the water," Harrison said. "Then, pass people if you are feeling good."

* Prepare to panic. "Pick an easy stroke, such as breaststroke, sidestroke or treading water that lets you rest and get your head above water while you collect yourself," said Surinsky.

* Practice navigating to stay on course. "Incorporate into your stroke a way to lift your head and look forward," said Erica Rose, one of the top open-water marathon swimmers in the U.S., who is currently training and studying at Northwestern. "Every 5 to 10 strokes, lift your head and 'sight' using a buoy or a landmark [as a reference point]." To put these tips to good use try one of the following events:

Swim Across America, July 21

Ninety percent of all the money raised for this non-competitive event will go toward fighting and treating cancer at the Cardinal Bernardin Cancer Center at Loyola University Medical Center. Choose from the half-mile or 1-mile courses that run parallel to the bike path, or the deeper 1.5- and 3-mile triangular courses. The fundraising minimum is $300 per person. For more info: swimacrossamerica.org.

Accenture Chicago Triathlon, Aug. 26

You will get kicked and pummeled during the crowded swim, which takes place parallel to the sea wall at Monroe Harbor. Expect to tread water at the start; the bottom is rocky and 6 to 8 feet deep. The main races closed on June 1, but check out chicagotriathlon.com to get in the back door.

Big Shoulders Open Water Swim Classic, Sept. 8

Always held on the first Sunday after Labor Day, this 5-kilometer Masters-sanctioned race starts at Ohio Street Beach and features two trips around a triangular course. Race-day entries and wetsuits are permitted, though wetsuit wearers will have their own division. For more info: bigshoulders.org.
